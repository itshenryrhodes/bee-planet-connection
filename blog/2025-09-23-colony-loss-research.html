Colony losses: what the latest research is (really) saying

Published: Tue 23 Sep 2025

Every beekeeper knows the sinking feeling of opening a hive in spring and finding it silent. Colony loss is nothing new – but the scale and drivers have been shifting in ways that are important for both hobbyists and commercial operations to understand.

The numbers

Surveys from Europe and North America suggest that winter losses in 2024–25 ran at around 15–20%, broadly in line with the long-term average. But what raised eyebrows this year was the uptick in summer losses – often attributed to forage gaps, heat stress and pesticide exposure. In some regions, reported combined annual losses were edging towards 35%.

Key drivers

  • Varroa destructor – still the number one stressor, with resistance to common treatments making management harder.
  • Forage and nutrition – patchy nectar flows and late frosts reduced key spring resources.
  • Weather extremes – hotter, drier summers stressed colonies and shortened flowering windows.
  • Pesticide exposure – sublethal doses continue to be implicated in reduced resilience.

What you can do

Research isn’t all doom and gloom. Practical steps can make a measurable difference:

  • Rotate and combine varroa treatments to reduce resistance build-up.
  • Plant or support continuous forage sources near your apiary – clover, borage, late ivy.
  • Ensure adequate shade and ventilation in hot spells.
  • Work with local farmer networks to share best practice around spray times and bee-safe treatments.

Why this matters

Bees aren’t just a hobby – they’re linchpins in our food system. Understanding colony loss trends helps us keep bees resilient, maintain pollination services, and support a sustainable apiculture sector. The science is clear: awareness plus action equals stronger bees.
