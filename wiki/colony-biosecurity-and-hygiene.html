Colony biosecurity & hygiene

Good hygiene and biosecurity reduce the risk of spreading disease and invasive pests such as small hive beetle. It’s a mix of common sense, discipline, and awareness of local regulations.

Key practices

  • Disinfect hive tools between colonies — a blowtorch or washing soda solution works well.
  • Avoid swapping comb or brood between colonies unless you’re certain they’re healthy.
  • Mark equipment clearly to avoid mixing between apiaries.
  • Clean and scorch second-hand equipment before use; never use old comb from unknown sources.

Apiary layout

  • Space hives to reduce drifting (which spreads mites and viruses).
  • Face entrances in different directions or use landmarks for bee orientation.

Deadouts & waste

Colonies that die out should be inspected carefully. Identify cause (starvation, Varroa, foulbrood) and dispose of contaminated comb responsibly.

Working with others

Talk to neighbouring beekeepers; coordinated management helps prevent reinfestation. Register colonies where required — inspectors can only help if they know where bees are kept.
