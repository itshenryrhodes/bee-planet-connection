Forage by season

A diverse forage calendar is vital for healthy colonies. Bees need both nectar (carbohydrate) and pollen (protein) throughout the year. In temperate regions, gaps in nectar flows can stress colonies and lead to starvation or dwindling brood.

Spring

  • Willow (Salix) – early pollen and nectar.
  • Fruit blossom (apple, pear, cherry, plum).
  • Dandelion – abundant pollen, early nectar.
  • Oilseed rape – major nectar source; pollen high in protein but prone to crystallisation in honey.

Summer

  • Clover (white, red).
  • Linden/lime trees (Tilia).
  • Borage – highly attractive, long flowering period.
  • Heather (Calluna vulgaris) – distinctive thixotropic honey.

Autumn

  • Ivy – late nectar and pollen; key for winter stores.
  • Michaelmas daisy.
  • Late brassicas and herbs in gardens.

Winter

  • Mahonia, snowdrops, crocus – early pollen/nectar on mild days.
  • Gorse – flowers almost year-round in some areas.

Planting for continuity

Aim to ensure something is flowering for bees from early spring through autumn. Mixed hedgerows, diverse gardens, and wildflower seedings help bridge “hungry gaps” between major flows.
