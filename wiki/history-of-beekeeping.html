History of beekeeping

Humans have harvested honey for millennia. Rock art from Spain (c. 8000 BCE) shows honey hunters collecting comb. Over time, crude honey hunting evolved into managed beekeeping with increasingly sophisticated hives.

Ancient practices

  • Egyptians: Clay and reed hives, honey used for food and medicine.
  • Greeks & Romans: Writings on bee biology; use of woven skeps and pottery hives.
  • China: Long history with Apis cerana, texts on management and medicinal honey.

Medieval to early modern

Skeps made of straw or wicker were common in Europe. Beeswax was valuable for candles, driving beekeeping demand. Management remained destructive: combs were cut out, often killing colonies.

The movable-frame revolution

In 1851, L.L. Langstroth popularised the movable-frame hive by defining “bee space”. This innovation allowed comb inspection without destroying colonies, transforming apiculture.

Modern developments

  • Proliferation of standardised hives (Langstroth, Dadant, National).
  • Queen rearing techniques for selective breeding.
  • Introduction of Varroa mites in the 20th century, changing management forever.
  • Recent innovations such as Flow frames and polyhives.

Cultural importance

Bees have long been symbols of industry, community and even divinity. Today, beekeeping continues to blend ancient tradition with cutting-edge science.
