Honey bee species & lineages

Honey bees belong to the genus Apis, with several recognised species. Two matter most to beekeepers: Apis mellifera (the Western honey bee), widely kept across Europe, Africa and the Americas; and Apis cerana (the Eastern honey bee), common across much of Asia. Within A. mellifera there are numerous subspecies (races) and regional lineages that reflect adaptation to local climates and flora.

Major Apis species (in brief)

  • Apis mellifera – the main managed species in Europe, Africa, the Middle East and the Americas.
  • Apis cerana – common in South and East Asia; smaller colonies; longstanding co-evolution with Varroa.
  • Apis dorsata – the giant honey bee of South/Southeast Asia; open-air nesting on cliff faces and tall trees.
  • Apis florea / Apis andreniformis – dwarf honey bees; small, exposed nests in bushes/trees.
  • Island/regionals (e.g., A. koschevnikovi, A. nigrocincta) with more limited ranges.

A. mellifera subspecies & lineages

Beekeepers often talk about morphological lineages within A. mellifera, shaped by ice-age refugia and migration:

Lineage M (Western Europe)

Includes A. m. mellifera (the “Dark European” bee). Adapted to cooler, maritime climates; historically common in Northern and Western Europe. Traits often cited: thriftiness, darker colouring, good wintering.

Lineage C (Southeast Europe)

Includes A. m. ligustica (Italian) and A. m. carnica (Carniolan). Popular in managed beekeeping. Traits often cited: gentle temperament, strong spring build-up, good for honey production.

Lineage A (Africa)

Diverse African subspecies (e.g., A. m. scutellata). High swarming propensity, heat tolerance, strong defensive behaviour in some populations.

Lineage O (Near East/Caucasus)

Includes A. m. caucasica. Noted for long tongues (good for deep nectaries), heavy propolis use, and calm temperament.

Common “named” stocks

In practice, many beekeepers use selected or hybridised stocks that emphasise productivity, gentleness and disease resistance: Italian, Carniolan, Caucasian, Buckfast, and locally selected “native-type” bees. Exact characteristics vary with breeder and environment.

Choosing bees for your context

  • Climate fit: Local adaptation often outperforms imported stock over the long term.
  • Temperament: Gentle bees reduce management stress, especially in urban settings.
  • Swarm tendency: If you can’t inspect frequently, opt for lines with moderate swarming.
  • Hygienic traits: Lines selected for hygiene can help with disease/varroa management.
